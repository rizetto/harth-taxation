The Contador de Resultas was the Chief Royal Accountant during the 17th and 18th centuries, and his duties were comparable to those of the Commissioner of Internal Revenue today. He served as the Chief Arbitrator, whose financial rulings were binding unless overturned by the Council of Indies. Indirect taxes on tobacco, alcohol, cockpits, burlas, and powder were some of the taxes that were levied on the populace, in addition to a tribute or head tax of one gold maiz per year and taxes on the value of jewels and gold trinkets. The tributo, which was once worth between 8 and 10 reales, is one of the taxes that date back to the Spanish Empire. Another system for paying taxes was polo y servicio or forced labor. Spain eventually replaced tributo with cedula, allowing them to keep track of the people who were able to pay taxes. Another way the Filipinos were taxed was through the encomienda system, which grants land ownership to deserving peninsulares. Other local taxes included the donativo de Zamboanga, which was levied expressly for the conquest of Jolo, and the vinta, which was paid by residents of the provinces along Western Luzon's coast to protect the region against the then-common Muslim pirates.
